# Delight

Delight is selective emotional emphasis. It makes the product feel responsive, intentional, and worth caring about.

## Main Principle

Do not spread delight evenly. Place it where it will be felt most.

## Baseline Requirement

Delight only works when the rest of the product feels cared for. If uncommon or low-traffic areas are sloppy, expressive moments elsewhere read as superficial.

Polish must feel holistic.

## Selective Emphasis

Choose moments that deserve extra attention:

- first-time setup
- successful completion of important tasks
- recovery from anxiety-producing moments
- infrequent features that would otherwise feel neglected
- moments of discovery

Delight can appear as:

- playful motion
- tactile response
- brief sound
- shimmer or ripple
- confetti or reward
- satisfying drag-and-drop behavior
- charming but unobtrusive empty-state guidance

## Delight-Impact Curve

Use delight intensity in rough proportion to usage frequency:

- **High-frequency features**: keep delight subtle, efficient, and non-blocking
- **Medium-frequency features**: allow modest surprise or discovery
- **Low-frequency features**: invest more; novelty lasts longer and the impact is greater

The same expressive flourish that feels magical once can become irritating when repeated daily.

## Surprise

Surprise is useful when:

- discovery feels safe
- the user can ignore it without penalty
- the feature is not hit constantly
- the interaction does not obstruct the task

Good surprise feels like a reward. Bad surprise feels like the product grabbing attention.

## Common vs Rare Features

### Common features

Use tiny details:

- number formatting that moves gracefully
- small state-aware micro-interactions
- subtle semantic feedback

Do not turn daily flows into performance art.

### Rare features

Invest more heavily:

- setup moments can feel ceremonial
- backup completion can feel rewarding
- destructive or cleanup actions can feel tactile and satisfying
- overlooked utility screens can carry a playful hidden layer

## Emotional Goal

Delight is not just entertainment. It shows that the product values the user's attention and emotional energy.

The goal is to transform necessary interactions from merely functional into memorable without sacrificing clarity.

## Failure Modes

- delight added everywhere with no hierarchy
- flashy moments masking poor fundamentals
- novelty repeated until it becomes friction
- delightful moments limited to obvious headline features while edge cases feel abandoned
- playful motion in high-stakes moments that should feel calm and trustworthy

## Review Prompts

- Is this feature used daily, weekly, rarely, or almost never?
- What level of delight will still feel good on the tenth use?
- Is this moment important enough to be remembered?
- Does this add warmth without stealing focus from the task?
- Are we neglecting lower-traffic surfaces that shape overall product perception?
